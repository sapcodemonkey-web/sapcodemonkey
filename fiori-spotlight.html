Spotlight · The frontend transition

Transaction codes became tiles. That changed more than the screen.

Users stopped memorising codes and started scanning a launchpad. Support stopped fielding "what's the t-code for…" and started fielding "why is my tile missing." Click any tile below to see what it replaced and what it did to both jobs.

Before

You had to know the code

Access was memory-based. Power users were fast because they'd learned dozens of codes; occasional users were slow, and they asked you. Expertise lived in people's heads.

After

You have to be given the tile

Access is assignment-based. If a tile isn't on someone's launchpad, the app effectively doesn't exist for them — even when their underlying authorisation is fine. Expertise moved into configuration.

The real change

The screen now tells you first

A dynamic tile shows a number before anyone opens anything. Users shift from "run a report to find out" to "glance, then act." That reshapes the working day more than the visual redesign did.

For support & Basis

Five things that land on your desk instead

The frontend moved, and the support workload moved with it — into configuration you now own.

01

"My tile is missing" replaces "what's the t-code"

The diagnosis path is new: is the app assigned to a catalogue, is the catalogue in a group or space, is the group assigned to the role, and does the underlying authorisation still hold? Four places to look where there used to be one.

02

Role design becomes front-end design

Classic role building was about what someone could execute. Now it also determines what they can see and how their day is laid out. Business catalogues and role assignment quietly became a user-experience decision made by IT.

03

A whole new layer can break

Gateway services, OData activation, caching, browser behaviour and theming sit between the user and the data now. "The app is blank" has causes that never existed in a GUI-only landscape.

04

Adoption becomes a support metric

If the launchpad is badly organised, people quietly fall back to whatever classic access still works — and the business concludes the migration failed. How tiles are grouped genuinely affects whether the project is judged a success.

05

Content updates arrive continuously

New and changed apps ship with releases, so the launchpad is never finished. Reviewing what changed, and deciding what to expose to whom, becomes a recurring task rather than a one-off project activity.
